Question: How does Newton define 'quantity of motion' and how is it distinct from 'force'?
Answer: In the Principia, Newton defines "quantity of motion" as the product of a body's mass and its velocity (momentum). It is distinct from "force," which Newton defines as an action that changes a body's state of motion (i.e., changes its quantity of motion). (Principia, Book I, Definitions I & II)

Question: According to Newton, what are the three laws of motion and how do they apply in celestial mechanics?
Answer: According to Newton in the *Principia*:

1. **First Law (Inertia):** A body remains at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by a force.
2. **Second Law (F=ma):** The change of motion (momentum) is proportional to the applied force and occurs in the direction of the force.
3. **Third Law (Action-Reaction):** For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

**Application in celestial mechanics:**  
Newton used these laws to explain planetary motion, showing that planets move in ellipses due to the gravitational force (an application of the second law), and that mutual gravitational attraction (third law) governs the motions of celestial bodies. The laws underpin his derivation of Kepler’s laws and the universal law of gravitation.

Question: How does Newton derive Kepler's Second Law (equal areas in equal times) from his laws of motion and gravitation?
Answer: Newton derives Kepler's Second Law by showing that a planet moving under a central force (like gravity) sweeps out equal areas in equal times because the force acts along the line joining the planet and the Sun. This means the planet’s angular momentum is conserved (Principia, Book 1, Proposition 1, Corollary 1). Thus, the area law follows directly from Newton’s laws of motion.

Question: How does Newton demonstrate that gravity is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between two masses?
Answer: Newton demonstrates the inverse-square law of gravity in Book I, Proposition 11 and Book III, Proposition 7 of the *Principia*. He shows that the observed planetary motions (Kepler’s laws), especially the elliptical orbits and the area law, require a force directed toward the Sun that decreases with the square of the distance. He mathematically proves that only an inverse-square force produces such orbits, thus establishing gravity’s proportionality to 1/r².

Question: What is Newton's argument for why gravitational force must act on all masses universally?
Answer: Newton argued that gravitational force must act on all masses universally because observations show that all bodies, regardless of composition, fall at the same rate and celestial motions (e.g., planets, moons) are governed by the same laws. In *Principia* (Book 3, Proposition 6, Corollary 2), he states that gravity "pertains to all bodies universally" and that this universality is confirmed by experiments and astronomical phenomena.

Question: How does Newton explain the perturbation of planetary orbits due to other planets?
Answer: Newton explains planetary perturbations as resulting from the mutual gravitational attractions between planets. In *Principia* (Book 1, Book 3), he shows that these additional forces cause deviations from perfect elliptical orbits, leading to irregularities such as changes in orbital speed and position. He analyzes these effects using his law of universal gravitation and provides mathematical methods for approximating their influence.

Question: What mathematical tools did Newton use in Principia that were precursors to calculus, and why didn't he use standard calculus notation?
Answer: In Principia, Newton used geometric methods—limits, ratios of vanishing quantities, and "fluxions" (his term for rates of change)—as precursors to calculus. He avoided standard calculus notation because it was not yet formalized or widely accepted; geometric proofs were considered more rigorous and traditional at the time.

Question: How does Newton use the concept of centripetal force to explain orbital motion?
Answer: Newton explains orbital motion by showing that a centripetal force, directed toward a central body (like the Sun), continually deflects a planet from a straight path, causing it to move in an ellipse. This force is necessary to maintain the planet’s curved orbit, as described in Book I of the *Principia*.

Question: How does Newton handle motion in resisting media, such as air or fluids?
Answer: In the Principia, Newton analyzes motion in resisting media by introducing resistance forces proportional to velocity or velocity squared. He shows that such resistance slows bodies, causing their motion to deviate from that in a vacuum, and provides mathematical solutions for these cases (Principia, Book II).

Question: In what way does Newton's notion of absolute space and time differ from relative motion, and how does it support his laws?
Answer: Newton’s absolute space and time are fixed, unchanging frameworks that exist independently of objects or events. Relative motion is movement measured between objects. Newton argued that his laws (especially inertia and gravity) require absolute space and time to define true motion (e.g., acceleration) and forces, not just motion relative to other bodies. This distinction is central in *Principia*, where he uses absolute space to explain phenomena like rotating buckets and planetary orbits.

Q1. How does Newton define 'quantity of motion' and how is it distinct from 'force'?
A1. Newton defines 'quantity of motion' as the measure arising from the velocity (celerity) multiplied by the quantity of matter (mass). It is the sum of the motions of all the parts of a body. In a body with double the mass and equal velocity, the quantity of motion is double; with twice the velocity, it is quadruple.

'Force' (specifically motive force) is distinct from quantity of motion; it arises from the accelerative force multiplied by the quantity of matter. While quantity of motion depends on mass and velocity, force depends on mass and acceleration.

Q2. According to Newton, what are the three laws of motion and how do they apply in celestial mechanics?
A2. Newtonâ€™s three laws of motion are:

1. First Law (Law of Inertia): An object remains at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external force.
2. Second Law (Law of Acceleration): The force acting on an object is equal to the mass of the object multiplied by its acceleration (F = ma).
3. Third Law (Action-Reaction): For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

In celestial mechanics, these laws explain planetary motion: planets move in orbits due to inertia (First Law), gravitational force causes their acceleration (Second Law), and the mutual gravitational attraction between celestial bodies demonstrates action and reaction (Third Law).

Q3. How does Newton derive Kepler's Second Law (equal areas in equal times) from his laws of motion and gravitation?
A3. Newton derives Kepler's Second Law (equal areas in equal times) using his laws of motion and universal gravitation by showing that a planet moving under the influence of the Sun's gravity experiences a central force directed toward the Sun. This central force causes the planet's angular momentum to be conserved. Conservation of angular momentum means that the line joining the planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal times, which is Kepler's Second Law. Key concepts: central force, conservation of angular momentum, equal areas in equal times.

Q4. How does Newton demonstrate that gravity is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between two masses?
A4. Newton demonstrates that gravity is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between two masses by showing that the force of gravity towards each particle of a body is inversely as the square of the distance from that particle. When considering two spheres with similar matter distributed equidistantly from their centers, the gravitational force between them is inversely as the square of the distance between their centers. This relationship is confirmed by observing the motions of celestial bodies, such as moons orbiting planets, which follow Keplerâ€™s laws and thus require a centripetal force that varies inversely with the square of the distance from the center. Newton further explains that the total gravitational force from a planet is the sum of the forces from all its parts, each following the inverse square law, and this law holds accurately at large distances and nearly so near the planetâ€™s surface.

Q5. What is Newton's argument for why gravitational force must act on all masses universally?
A5. Gravitational force must act on all masses universally because experiments and astronomical observations show that all bodies gravitate towards each other in proportion to the quantity of matter they contain. The force of gravity between any two bodies is proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers. This mutual gravitation applies to all bodiesâ€”planets, moons, comets, and every particle of matterâ€”demonstrating that universal gravitation is a property of all matter, not limited to specific objects.

Q6. How does Newton explain the perturbation of planetary orbits due to other planets?
A6. Newton explains the perturbation of planetary orbits as the result of gravitational forces exerted by other planets. These mutual gravitational attractions cause deviations from perfect elliptical orbits, leading to irregularities or perturbations in the motion of each planet. The superposition of these forces alters the planets' velocities and positions over time, resulting in complex orbital paths rather than simple ellipses.

Q7. What mathematical tools did Newton use in Principia that were precursors to calculus, and why didn't he use standard calculus notation?
A7. Newton used the method of fluxions, which is a precursor to calculus, in the Principia. He introduced the fundamental principle of his fluxionary calculus in the second Lemma of the second book. Newton did not use standard calculus notation because he admired the taste and form of demonstration of the ancient geometers and preferred their style. He regretted not following the ancients more closely and was reluctant to publish his purely mathematical writings, partly due to his repugnance to contest or dispute.

Q8. How does Newton use the concept of centripetal force to explain orbital motion?
A8. Newton explains orbital motion by stating that a centripetal force acts continuously on a body moving in a curved path, such as a planet orbiting the Sun. This centripetal force pulls the body toward the center of its orbit, preventing it from moving in a straight line due to inertia. In planetary motion, gravity provides the necessary centripetal force, causing the planet to follow an elliptical orbit around the Sun. The balance between the planetâ€™s inertia (tendency to move in a straight line) and the gravitational centripetal force results in stable orbital motion.

Q9. How does Newton handle motion in resisting media, such as air or fluids?
A9. Newton explains that the resistance experienced by bodies moving in fluids is nearly proportional to the density of the fluid and the square of the velocity of the body. He notes that lighter fluids like air offer less resistance than denser ones like water or mercury, in direct proportion to their densities. The resistance in fluids arises from three sources: the inactivity of the fluid matter, the tenacity of its parts, and friction. For sufficiently fluid substances, resistance is mainly due to density and velocity squared. Newton also distinguishes that more tenacious fluids resist more than less tenacious ones, even if their densities are equal. He provides methods to determine the density of the medium and the resistance encountered by a body moving along a given curve, and confirms his theory with experiments, such as those involving pendulums.

Q10. In what way does Newton's notion of absolute space and time differ from relative motion, and how does it support his laws?
A10. Newton's notion of absolute space and time asserts that space and time exist independently and uniformly, providing a fixed, unchanging reference frame. Absolute space is the same everywhere and always, unaffected by objects or events, while absolute time flows uniformly regardless of anything external. In contrast, relative motion describes the movement of objects only in relation to each other, not to any fixed background.

Newton's absolute space and time support his laws by providing a universal frame in which his laws of motionâ€”especially inertia and accelerationâ€”can be defined objectively. This allows for the distinction between true motion (motion relative to absolute space) and apparent motion (motion relative to other objects), ensuring the consistency and universality of Newton's laws.

A11. Isaac Newton's grandfather was Robert Newton.

A12. No, Isaac Newton did not have any descendants. He never married and had no children, so he left no direct lineage.

Q1. How does Newton define 'quantity of motion' and how is it distinct from 'force'?
A1. Quantity of motion is defined as the product of the quantity of matter (mass) and celerity (velocity) of a body. It is distinct from force in that quantity of motion measures the motion itself, while force refers to the action that changes or generates motion. Motive force arises from the accelerative force multiplied by the quantity of matter, whereas quantity of motion arises from velocity multiplied by the quantity of matter.

Q2. According to Newton, what are the three laws of motion and how do they apply in celestial mechanics?
A2. Newton‚Äôs three laws of motion are:

1. First Law (Law of Inertia): An object remains at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external force.
2. Second Law (Law of Acceleration): The force acting on an object is equal to the mass of the object multiplied by its acceleration (F = ma).
3. Third Law (Law of Action and Reaction): For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

In celestial mechanics, these laws explain planetary motion, orbital dynamics, and gravitational interactions. The first law describes how planets move in stable orbits unless influenced by external forces. The second law quantifies how gravitational force causes acceleration of celestial bodies. The third law governs mutual gravitational attraction, ensuring that forces between two bodies, such as a planet and the Sun, are equal and opposite.

Q3. How does Newton derive Kepler's Second Law (equal areas in equal times) from his laws of motion and gravitation?
A3. Newton derives Kepler's Second Law (equal areas in equal times) using his laws of motion and universal gravitation by showing that a planet moving under the influence of the Sun's gravitational force experiences a central force directed toward the Sun. A central force produces zero torque about the center, so angular momentum is conserved. Conservation of angular momentum means the line joining the planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal times. This directly leads to Kepler's Second Law.

Q4. How does Newton demonstrate that gravity is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between two masses?
A4. Newton demonstrates that gravity is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between two masses by showing that the force of gravity towards each particle of a body is reciprocally as the square of the distance from that particle. When considering two spheres with similar matter distributed equidistantly from their centers, the gravitational force between them is inversely as the square of the distance between their centers. This relationship is confirmed by observing the motions of celestial bodies, such as moons orbiting planets, which follow the same law: their centripetal forces are inversely proportional to the squares of their distances from the center. Thus, the total gravitational force between two masses is the sum of the forces from all their parts, each following the inverse square law.

Q5. What is Newton's argument for why gravitational force must act on all masses universally?
A5. Gravitational force must act on all masses universally because experiments and astronomical observations show that all bodies gravitate towards each other in proportion to the quantity of matter they contain. The moon gravitates towards the earth, the sea towards the moon, all planets towards each other, and comets towards the sun. This mutual gravitation is proportional to the matter in each body and acts between every particle of matter. The force of gravity between two bodies is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers. Therefore, all bodies are endowed with a principle of mutual gravitation, and gravity acts universally on all masses.

Q6. How does Newton explain the perturbation of planetary orbits due to other planets?
A6. Newton explains the perturbation of planetary orbits as the result of gravitational forces exerted by other planets. These mutual gravitational attractions cause deviations from perfect elliptical orbits, leading to irregularities or disturbances in the motion of each planet. The superposition of these gravitational influences alters the orbital path, resulting in observable perturbations.

Q7. What mathematical tools did Newton use in Principia that were precursors to calculus, and why didn't he use standard calculus notation?
A7. Newton used geometric methods and his fluxionary calculus as mathematical tools in Principia, which were precursors to calculus. He did not use standard calculus notation because he admired the taste and form of ancient geometric demonstration, preferred their style, and regretted not following them more closely. He also had a repugnance to contest or dispute, which contributed to his reluctance to publish his purely mathematical writings and adopt new notations.

Q8. How does Newton use the concept of centripetal force to explain orbital motion?
A8. Newton explains orbital motion by stating that a centripetal force acts continuously on a body moving in a curved path, such as a planet orbiting the Sun. This force is always directed toward the center of the orbit, causing the body to accelerate toward that center and thus maintain its curved, orbital trajectory. In planetary motion, gravity provides the necessary centripetal force, keeping planets in stable orbits around the Sun.

Q9. How does Newton handle motion in resisting media, such as air or fluids?
A9. Newton explains that the resistance experienced by bodies moving in fluids is nearly proportional to the density of the medium and the square of the velocity. He distinguishes between different types of resistance‚Äîarising from the inactivity of the fluid, tenacity of its parts, or friction. In sufficiently fluid media like air, water, or mercury, resistance is mainly due to density and velocity squared. He also notes that more tenacious fluids resist more than less viscous ones of equal density. Newton analyzes how resistance affects motion, provides methods to determine the density of the medium for a given motion, and confirms his theory with experiments, such as those involving pendulums.

Q10. In what way does Newton's notion of absolute space and time differ from relative motion, and how does it support his laws?
A10. Newton's notion of absolute space and time asserts that space and time exist independently and uniformly, providing a fixed, unchanging reference frame. Absolute space is the true, immovable framework within which all motion occurs, while absolute time flows uniformly regardless of external factors. Relative motion, in contrast, is measured only with respect to other objects and does not reveal true motion. Newton's laws, especially the first law (inertia) and second law (F=ma), require absolute space and time to define true acceleration and force, distinguishing real motion from mere relative motion between bodies.

Q11. Who was the grandfather of Isaac Newton?
A11. Isaac Newton's paternal grandfather was Robert Newton, and his maternal grandfather was James Ayscough.

Q12. Do we know any other descent of Isaac Newton apart from his grandfather?
A12. No, Isaac Newton did not have any descendants. He never married and had no children, so he left no direct lineage.

Q1. How does Newton define 'quantity of motion' and how is it distinct from 'force'?
A1. Quantity of motion is defined as the product of the quantity of matter (mass) and celerity (velocity) of a body. It is distinct from force in that quantity of motion measures the motion itself, while force refers to the action that changes or tends to change the motion. Motive force arises from the accelerative force multiplied by the quantity of matter, whereas quantity of motion arises from velocity multiplied by the quantity of matter.

Q2. According to Newton, what are the three laws of motion and how do they apply in celestial mechanics?
A2. Newton‚Äôs three laws of motion are:

1. First Law (Law of Inertia): An object remains at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external force.
2. Second Law (Law of Acceleration): The force acting on an object is equal to the mass of the object multiplied by its acceleration (F = ma).
3. Third Law (Action-Reaction): For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

In celestial mechanics, these laws explain planetary motion, orbital dynamics, and gravitational interactions. The first law describes how planets move in stable orbits unless influenced by external forces. The second law quantifies how gravitational force causes acceleration of celestial bodies. The third law governs mutual gravitational attraction, ensuring that forces between two bodies, such as a planet and the Sun, are equal and opposite.

Q3. How does Newton derive Kepler's Second Law (equal areas in equal times) from his laws of motion and gravitation?
A3. Newton derives Kepler's Second Law (equal areas in equal times) using his laws of motion and universal gravitation by showing that a planet moving under the influence of the Sun's gravitational force experiences a central force directed toward the Sun. A central force produces zero torque about the center, so angular momentum is conserved. Conservation of angular momentum means the line joining the planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal times. This directly leads to Kepler's Second Law.

Q4. How does Newton demonstrate that gravity is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between two masses?
A4. Newton demonstrates that gravity is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between two masses by showing that the force of gravity towards each particle of a body is reciprocally as the square of the distance from that particle. For two spheres with similar matter distributed equidistantly from their centers, the gravitational force between them is inversely as the square of the distance between their centers. This relationship is confirmed by observing that the centripetal forces required to keep moons or planets in orbit follow the same inverse square law, as seen in the periodic times of their orbits. Thus, the total gravitational force between two masses is the sum of the forces from all their parts, each following the inverse square law, resulting in the overall force being inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers.

Q5. What is Newton's argument for why gravitational force must act on all masses universally?
A5. Gravitational force must act on all masses universally because experiments and astronomical observations show that all bodies gravitate towards each other in proportion to the quantity of matter they contain. The moon gravitates towards the earth, the sea towards the moon, all planets towards each other, and comets towards the sun. The force of gravity between any two bodies is proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers. This universal gravitation applies to every particle of matter, meaning all bodies are endowed with a principle of mutual gravitation.

Q6. How does Newton explain the perturbation of planetary orbits due to other planets?
A6. Newton explains the perturbation of planetary orbits as the result of gravitational forces exerted by other planets. These mutual gravitational attractions cause deviations from perfect elliptical orbits, leading to irregularities such as changes in orbital speed, direction, and position. The superposition of these gravitational influences results in complex, non-Keplerian motion, known as orbital perturbations.

Q7. What mathematical tools did Newton use in Principia that were precursors to calculus, and why didn't he use standard calculus notation?
A7. Newton used geometric methods and his fluxionary calculus as mathematical tools in Principia, which were precursors to calculus. He did not use standard calculus notation because he admired the taste and form of ancient geometric demonstration, preferred their style, and regretted not following them more closely. He also had a repugnance to contest or dispute, which contributed to his reluctance to publish his purely mathematical writings and adopt new notations.

Q8. How does Newton use the concept of centripetal force to explain orbital motion?
A8. Newton explains orbital motion by stating that a centripetal force acts continuously on a body moving in a curved path, such as a planet orbiting the Sun. This force is always directed toward the center of the orbit, causing the body to accelerate toward that center and thus maintain its curved trajectory. In planetary motion, gravity provides the necessary centripetal force, keeping planets in stable orbits around the Sun.

Q9. How does Newton handle motion in resisting media, such as air or fluids?
A9. Newton explains that the resistance experienced by bodies moving in fluids is nearly proportional to the density of the medium and the square of the velocity. He distinguishes between different types of resistance‚Äîarising from the inactivity of the fluid, tenacity of its parts, or friction. In sufficiently fluid media like air, water, and certain oils, resistance is mainly due to density and velocity squared. He also notes that more tenacious fluids resist more than less viscous ones of equal density. Newton provides methods to determine the density of the medium and the resistance encountered by a body moving along a given path, and confirms his theory with experiments, such as those involving pendulums.

Q10. In what way does Newton's notion of absolute space and time differ from relative motion, and how does it support his laws?
A10. Newton's notion of absolute space and time asserts that space and time exist independently and uniformly, providing a fixed, unchanging framework. Absolute space is the immovable stage where all motion occurs, and absolute time flows uniformly regardless of events. Relative motion, in contrast, describes movement only in relation to other objects, not to this fixed background. Newton's laws, especially the first law (inertia), rely on absolute space and time to define true motion and rest, distinguishing them from merely relative changes in position. This framework ensures that forces and accelerations are measured objectively, supporting the universality and consistency of his laws of motion.

- Costs associated with acquisition of the donor‚Äôs organ.
- Medical expenses for experimental or investigational organ transplants.
- Any medical treatment or complication in respect of the donor, consequent to harvesting.
- Expenses related to organ transportation or preservation.
